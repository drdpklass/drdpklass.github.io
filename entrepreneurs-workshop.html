01
Discover

Find a problem worth your next two years

Every venture that lasts starts from a problem someone can describe in one sentence, not a technology looking for a use. Your job here is to go looking, not to wait for inspiration.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Spend 30 minutes writing down every annoyance, delay, or workaround you personally hit this week.
  2. Ask ten people outside your friend circle what wastes their time or money regularly.
  3. Spend one hour just observing a market, a queue, or a canteen without asking anyone anything.
  4. Sort your best three to five problem candidates into Local Problem / Ease of Living / Start-up Idea.
  5. Pick one and run the 5-Whys on it until you reach the real root cause.
  6. Write the final problem statement in a single, specific sentence.

Where to lookFour honest sources of ideas

  • Your own friction. What did you personally have to work around this month? Annoyance is data.
  • Your community's friction. Ask ten people outside your friend group what wastes their time or money weekly.
  • Local systems that creak. Look at agriculture, transport, healthcare access, waste, local commerce, and government service delivery near you — these are the categories judges and funders keep funding.
  • Watching, not asking. People are bad at describing their own problems but excellent at showing them. Spend an hour observing a market, a clinic queue, or a college canteen with nothing to do but watch.

Sort itWhich bucket is it in?

Almost every competition brief and grant call sorts problems into the same three lanes. Know which one you're in before you start writing.

  • Local problem a specific pain point in a specific community
  • Ease of living / doing business something that makes an everyday or business task simpler
  • Start-up / business idea a concept with a real path to revenue

Do thisThe 5-Whys, in one sitting

Write the problem in one sentence. Ask "why does this happen?" Answer it. Ask why again about that answer. Do this five times. Most teams discover the problem they started with was a symptom, and the real, fundable problem was two or three "whys" deeper.

02
Ideate

Generate more solutions than you need, then cut hard

The first idea is rarely the best one — it's just the first one that occurred to you. Force volume before you fall in love with anything.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Set a 20-minute timer and write 15 different ways to solve your chosen problem.
  2. Take your top three ideas and run each through a SCAMPER pass.
  3. For each surviving idea, ask: could a version exist this week, for free, using just a phone?
  4. Score every idea 1–5 on originality, feasibility, impact and scalability — or use the Idea Validation Scorer in the Toolkit.
  5. Add up the scores and commit to the highest-scoring idea.

Quantity first

Set a timer for 20 minutes and write 15 different ways to solve the problem, including the obviously bad ones. Judgement kills volume — do it after, not during.

SCAMPER pass

Take your best three ideas and Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to another use, Eliminate, or Reverse a part of each. This alone usually produces the version you actually build.

Constraint test

Could a first version exist with zero budget, in one week, using only a phone? If nothing survives that question, simplify until something does.

Pick with a rule, not a feeling. Score every candidate idea 1–5 on originality, feasibility with what you actually have, potential impact, and how easily it could grow. Add the numbers. The highest score is what you build first — or use the Idea Validation Scorer in the Founder's Toolkit below to automate this.

03
Validate

Prove it before you build it

A proposal that has never met a real user is a guess with good formatting. Validation is cheap; a wrong build is not.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. List the ten people you will interview — none of them your friends or family.
  2. Ask each what they currently do about the problem and what it costs them, not whether they'd use your idea.
  3. Identify the single assumption that would kill the idea if it turned out false.
  4. Design the cheapest possible test for that one assumption.
  5. Build a paper prototype, concierge test, or landing page and run it with five real users this week.

Talk to strangers, not friends

Friends and family say yes to be kind. Find ten people who actually experience the problem and are unrelated to you. Ask what they currently do about it, what they've tried, and what it costs them — never ask "would you use this?", ask what they do today.

Name the killer risk

Every idea has one assumption that, if false, ends it. Is it that people will pay? That the technology works at scale? That a supplier will cooperate? Identify it honestly and test that one thing first, cheaply.

Build smallPrototype before product

  • Paper prototype — sketch every screen or step on paper and walk a real user through it.
  • Concierge test — do the service manually for five real users before automating anything.
  • Landing page test — describe the offer on one page and see if strangers actually click "notify me" or "reserve."

04
Plan & write

Write the proposal the way evaluators actually read it

Whether it's a college-level competition, a state programme, or an investor memo, the skeleton underneath is nearly always the same six parts. Master this once.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Draft the title, category, and one-line problem statement first.
  2. Write the proposed solution section, tying it directly back to that problem.
  3. Fill in feasibility, scalability, and impact honestly, not optimistically.
  4. Write a dated, month-by-month implementation plan for the next six months.
  5. Compress everything into a synopsis of about 500 words.
  6. Get a mentor or teacher to read it and countersign the originality declaration.

TemplateThe six-part proposal skeleton

SectionWhat it must answer
Title & categoryOne line naming the idea, and which lane it sits in (local problem / ease of living / start-up)
Problem statementWho hurts, how badly, how often, backed by what you actually heard from users
Proposed solutionWhat you'll build and why it fixes the specific pain point named above
Feasibility, scalability & impactCan it be built with realistic resources, can it grow beyond the pilot, who benefits and by how much
Implementation planWhat happens in month 1, 3, 6 — concrete, dated, ownable steps
SynopsisAll of the above compressed to a version a busy reader finishes in two minutes

Respect the word limit

Most institutional formats cap the full proposal around 2,000 words and ask for a tight synopsis of about 500 words. Write the synopsis last, once the full version exists — it's a distillation, not a preview.

Get it countersigned

Institutional programmes typically require a self-declaration of originality, countersigned by a guiding teacher or mentor. Loop your mentor in early — a rushed signature the night before a deadline is a bad first impression.

05
Structure & legal

Give the idea a legal body before it grows a public face

You don't need a lawyer on day one, but you do need to know which structure fits your stage — the wrong one costs far more to undo later than to choose correctly now.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Identify your current stage: testing alone, co-founding, or ready to raise or hire.
  2. Match that stage to a legal structure using the comparison table below.
  3. Check whether your turnover or activity already requires Udyam/MSME or GST registration.
  4. Decide if your brand name needs early trademark protection.
  5. Draft and sign a founders' agreement covering equity, roles, and an exit clause.

ChooseLegal structures compared

StructureLiabilityBest suited for
Sole proprietorshipPersonal, unlimitedTesting an idea alone with minimal paperwork
Partnership / LLPLimited in an LLPTwo or more founders sharing work and risk formally
Private Limited CompanyLimited to the companyRaising equity funding, hiring at scale, building long-term credibility

Registrations to know about

  • Udyam / MSME registration — unlocks government scheme eligibility, easier loans, and tender access
  • GST registration — required once turnover crosses the prescribed threshold, or if you sell across state lines
  • Trademark — protects your brand name and logo; file early if the name is central to your value
  • Shop & establishment / trade licence — local municipal requirement for a physical outlet or office

Contracts every founder needs

  • A founders' agreement — equity split, roles, what happens if someone leaves
  • An NDA for sensitive conversations with vendors or early hires
  • A simple terms-of-service and privacy note the moment you take a customer's data
  • Written vendor and freelancer contracts — a verbal deal is not a deal
Rule of thumb: register the minimum structure your current stage actually needs. Over-engineering compliance before you have a customer wastes money; under-registering once you have revenue invites penalties. Revisit this decision at every funding round.

06
Fund

Match the ask to the stage you're actually at

Most student founders ask for too much, too early, from the wrong door. Funding comes in a ladder — climb it rung by rung.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Work out the real cost of building your prototype before deciding how much to ask for.
  2. List three funding routes you could realistically pursue: bootstrap, competition/grant, or investor.
  3. Gather the paperwork in advance: joint bank account, ID proofs, cancelled cheque, mentor declaration.
  4. Map your budget only to permitted spending categories before you apply.
  5. Apply to the most appropriate rung of the funding ladder for your current stage.

Rung 1 — Bootstrap

Your own savings, family, free campus resources, and free-tier software. This is how you afford to fail cheaply and often before anyone else's money is involved.

Rung 2 — Institutional competitions & grants

College, university, and state-level innovation competitions, incubator seed grants, and government schemes. Non-dilutive — you keep full ownership.

Rung 3 — Angel & VC

Comes later, once there's a working product and early traction. Dilutive — you trade equity for capital and speed.

Worked exampleHow one real state programme structures this ladder

Odisha's Higher Education Department runs its Innovation and Start-Up Competition exactly as a three-round funnel — a useful pattern to recognise wherever you're studying, because most state and national programmes echo it.

Competition funnel & typical support
RoundWho advancesWhat's on offer
Institution levelEvery enrolled student — participation is treated as the norm, not the exceptionCertificates for all; top 3 proposals move up
University levelTop 3 proposals from every affiliated college, evaluated by a panel including an external expertTop 7 proposals receive prototype funding, paid in instalments
State levelProposals recommended by universities, reviewed by academia, industry and government expertsTop 10 proposals receive larger prototype grants, mentorship, and incubator access

Prototype-grant instalments, in practice

Programmes like this rarely hand over the full amount at once — funds are released against progress. This is the shape you should plan a budget around, whichever grant you eventually win:

StageTypical shareUnlocked by
Kick-off~20–40%Signed agreement + a concrete plan
Mid-build~25–40%A progress report showing real work done
Completion~20–30%A finished, verified prototype

Paperwork to prepare in advance

  • Joint bank account (student lead + faculty mentor) — many grants pay only into a joint account, never a personal one
  • Aadhaar and PAN (or local equivalent ID) for every account holder
  • A cancelled cheque or account-verification proof
  • A signed mentor declaration of originality

Having these ready before you're asked is the single fastest way to avoid losing weeks to a disbursement delay.

Funds are never yours to spend freely. Grant money is almost always ring-fenced to product development, equipment, testing, certification, and initial market entry — not personal expenses, not unrelated activity. Misuse routinely triggers a refund demand within a fixed window (commonly 60 days), and can mean disqualification from future rounds. Build your budget only around permitted categories.

07
Build & operate

Winning the grant is the start of a new job: delivery

Every rupee of grant money comes with an accountability trail, and every product needs someone actually running it day to day. Treat both like a job, not a prize.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Sign the agreement or MoU before spending a single rupee.
  2. Open a dedicated folder — physical or digital — to store every bill and receipt from day one.
  3. Write a one-page SOP for the task you'll repeat most often.
  4. Put your quarterly or milestone review dates directly into your calendar.
  5. Submit your first progress report on or before the deadline, with evidence attached.

DoPermitted use of funds

  • Product development and procurement of materials
  • Equipment, tools, and software licences
  • Testing, validation, and certification costs
  • Business registration and first market-entry activity
  • Field trials and pilot implementation

Don'tWhat gets funds clawed back

  • Personal expenses of any kind
  • Spend unrelated to the funded project
  • Any unapproved or undocumented purchase
  • Missed progress reports or skipped review meetings
  • False reporting of milestones reached

Operations basics no one tells you

  • Write a one-page SOP for any task you'll repeat more than five times
  • Pick vendors on reliability first, price second — a cheap vendor who misses deadlines costs more
  • Track inventory or service capacity in a single spreadsheet before reaching for software
  • Decide your quality-check step before your first real order, not after the first complaint

Report on a rhythm, not on demand

Larger grants typically run on a quarterly review committee — expect to show physical progress, financial utilisation, milestone achievement, and a mentor's sign-off at each checkpoint. Build these dates into your calendar the day you receive the funds, not the week the report is due.

Who's responsible for what

RoleOwns
Student teamExecution, timely reporting, spending exactly per the approved plan
Faculty mentorTechnical/business guidance, joint fund monitoring, certifying progress
Institution / universityQuarterly review, infrastructure, incubation access
State body / departmentApproving and releasing funds, state-level evaluation and audit

Every rupee needs a paper trail

Keep every bill and voucher from day one. Grants are routinely subject to audit, sometimes by an independent third party. Non-compliance can mean disqualification or blacklisting from future programmes — a cost far higher than the admin effort of good bookkeeping.

08
Numbers & finance

An entrepreneur who can't read their own numbers is guessing

You don't need an MBA to run the numbers that matter. You need three habits: know your unit economics, watch cash — not just profit, and keep records clean enough to defend.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. List every cost that goes into producing or delivering one unit.
  2. Set your price so that price minus cost leaves a clear, positive margin.
  3. Calculate your break-even units using the calculator in the Founder's Toolkit.
  4. Build a simple weekly cash-in / cash-out log, kept separate from your profit calculation.
  5. Project your cash flow for the next three months using realistic, not hopeful, numbers.

Unit economics, in one line

For every single unit you sell: Price − Cost to make/deliver = Margin. If that number isn't clearly positive on paper, no amount of volume fixes it — you'll just lose money faster.

Break-even, in one line

Fixed costs ÷ Margin per unit = Units needed to break even. Try the calculator in the Founder's Toolkit below with your own numbers.

Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact. A business can show a profit on paper and still run out of money if customers pay late and suppliers must be paid now. Track cash in and cash out weekly, separately from your profit calculation, from day one.

Keep it simpleThree records every founder should maintain

  • A cash flow log — every rupee in, every rupee out, updated weekly
  • A simple profit & loss — revenue minus costs, by month
  • A running list of what you owe and what's owed to you — the two numbers that quietly kill under-managed businesses

09
Team & culture

No venture scales on one person's hours alone

Whether it's a co-founder, an intern, or your first full-time hire — how you build the team decides how far the idea can travel.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. List the skills your idea needs that you don't personally have.
  2. If you're considering a co-founder, test the working relationship on one small real task first.
  3. Rate each founder's contribution honestly and agree an equity split in writing — the Toolkit's Equity Split Calculator is a good starting point.
  4. Add a vesting schedule so equity is earned over time, not handed over on day one.
  5. Write your non-negotiable values on one page before you hire anyone.
  6. Define your next role clearly before you advertise it.

Picking a co-founder

  • Look for complementary skills, not a copy of yourself
  • Test the working relationship on a small real project before committing long-term
  • Agree on shared values around effort, honesty, and conflict before agreeing on the idea

Splitting equity fairly

Split by contribution, not friendship — time committed, capital put in, and unique skill brought all count. Add a vesting schedule (equity earned gradually over time) so a co-founder who leaves in month two doesn't keep a founder's share forever.

HiringYour first employee or intern

  • Write the role down before you post it — vague roles attract vague candidates
  • Hire for attitude and trainability early; you can teach a specific tool faster than you can teach ownership
  • Put every hire's pay, hours, and responsibilities in writing, even for an unpaid internship
Culture is what you tolerate, not what you write on a wall. Decide early what you'll never compromise on — quality, honesty with customers, how you treat vendors — and hold yourself to it first.

10
Marketing & sales

A great product nobody knows about isn't a business yet

You don't need to be everywhere. You need to be exactly where your first hundred customers already are, saying one clear thing.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Fill in the positioning sentence — "For [who], who [problem], [product] is the [category] that [benefit] — unlike [alternative]" — or use the Elevator Pitch tool in the Toolkit.
  2. Identify the single channel where your exact users already spend time.
  3. Show up there consistently for two weeks before judging the results.
  4. Personally reach out to your first 10–20 potential customers yourself.
  5. Ask every customer who says no, why — and adjust your pitch accordingly.

Position it in one sentence

"For [who], who [problem], [product] is the [category] that [key benefit] — unlike [alternative]." If you can't fill this in cleanly, your offer isn't clear enough to sell yet.

Pick one channel first

Go where your specific users already spend time — a WhatsApp community, a college notice board, a local market, or one social platform — and win that fully before spreading thin across five channels at once.

The funnelFive stages every customer moves through

StageYour job
AwarenessBe seen where they already look
InterestSay the one clear benefit, fast
TrialMake the first try low-risk and low-effort
PurchaseRemove every friction point at the moment of paying
LoyaltyFollow up after the sale — repeat customers cost far less than new ones
In the beginning, you are the sales team. Founder-led sales — personally talking to your first customers, handling their objections, and closing the deal yourself — teaches you what no market research report can. Don't outsource this too early.

11
Pitch & raise

Win the room in ten slides and three minutes

A pitch is not a summary of your document read aloud — it's a different artifact built to be heard, not read. And if you're raising equity, know the basic language before you sit at that table.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Write one slide for each of the ten sections in the deck skeleton.
  2. Rehearse your three-minute verbal version out loud, timed, at least five times.
  3. Write out answers to the five toughest questions in advance.
  4. If raising equity, look up your target investor's typical cheque size and terms beforehand.
  5. Ask a mentor or peer to pitch-test you, and take their harshest feedback seriously.

DeckTen slides, in order

  1. The problem, told as a real moment, not a statistic
  2. Who exactly it happens to, and how often
  3. Your solution, in one sentence
  4. How it actually works (a demo beats a description)
  5. Evidence you talked to real users
  6. Why now / why you
  7. What "grown up" looks like — the scale case
  8. Milestones already hit
  9. What you're asking for, and exactly what it buys
  10. The team and how to reach you

What judges actually score

This is the exact rubric shape used in institutional evaluations — memorise the weighting, not just the categories.

CriterionWeight
Originality30%
Impact potential25%
Feasibility20%
Scalability15%
Presentation10%

Notice originality and impact together outweigh a polished slide deck. Substance beats gloss — but don't skip rehearsal either.

Investor basicsWords you should understand before you raise equity

  • Valuation — what the company is agreed to be worth before/after the investment
  • Cap table — the full record of who owns what percentage of the company
  • Term sheet — the non-binding summary of a deal's terms before final legal contracts
  • Convertible note / SAFE — early-stage instruments that turn into equity later, at a future valuation
  • Red flag: any investor unwilling to explain a term simply is not one you want on your cap table

See the full Founder's Glossary for more terms.

Five questions a judge or investor will almost certainly ask
  • "Who is your first paying or first served customer, specifically?"
  • "What have you already built, versus what's still a plan?"
  • "What happens to this idea if I say no today?"
  • "What's your biggest unresolved risk?"
  • "Why is your team the right one to execute this?"
Common pitch mistakes to cut before you present
  • Opening with your name and college instead of the problem
  • Reading slides word for word instead of talking to the room
  • No visible evidence you've spoken to a real user
  • A funding ask with no breakdown of what it buys
  • Running over time — judges stop listening the moment you do

12
Scale, mindset & legacy

Turn a funded prototype into a lasting venture

A completed prototype is a milestone, not a finish line. The founders who compound are the ones who manage their own mind as carefully as their business, and start pulling others up with them.

Do this, in orderStep-by-step for this station

  1. Write down your outcome in numbers: users reached, revenue earned, impact created.
  2. Decide, in advance, what evidence would tell you to pivot or to stop.
  3. Apply to the next competition round or an incubator this month.
  4. Schedule one hour a week purely for your own rest and reflection.
  5. Find one junior student to mentor through Station 01 this term.

Document the outcome

Product built, users reached, revenue or measurable impact, jobs created if any. Funded teams are commonly expected to become documented success stories — write yours down properly, with numbers.

Keep climbing

Re-enter the next level of competition, apply to a university or state-funded incubation centre, and use priority access to labs and co-working space where it's offered.

Mentor the next cohort

The fastest way to deepen your own understanding of a stage is to guide someone else through it. Judges and reviewers notice founders who give back.

MindsetWhat no proposal template teaches you

  • Decide with the information you have. Waiting for certainty is itself a decision — usually the wrong one.
  • Separate the failed experiment from a failed founder. A pivot backed by evidence is progress, not defeat.
  • Know your shut-down line in advance. Decide, before you're emotionally exhausted, what evidence would tell you to stop or change direction.
  • Protect your own rest. A founder who never recovers makes worse decisions, not more of them.
  • Keep learning on purpose. Read outside your field, stay close to mentors, and revisit this whole route again at your next stage — every station looks different with revenue in the bank.
Certificates and recognition compound too. Participation certificates, formal recommendation to the next level, mentor endorsements, and public success-story write-ups all become part of your track record — the same portfolio that gets the next incubator, the next investor meeting, or the next scholarship to say yes faster.

Reference

The founder's glossary

The vocabulary that lets you sit in any funding or strategy conversation without nodding blankly.

MVP

Minimum Viable Product — the smallest version of your idea that still lets you learn from real users.

CAC

Customer Acquisition Cost — what it costs, on average, to win one paying customer.

LTV

Lifetime Value — the total revenue you can expect from one customer over the life of the relationship.

Runway

How many months your business can operate before the cash runs out, at the current spending rate.

Burn rate

How fast you're spending cash each month, before revenue is netted off.

ARR / MRR

Annual / Monthly Recurring Revenue — predictable revenue you can count on repeating.

Equity

Ownership share in the company, usually expressed as a percentage.

Valuation

The agreed worth of the company, used to decide how much equity an investment buys.

Cap table

The full record of who owns what percentage of the company, and how that changes with each round.

Vesting

Equity earned gradually over time rather than all at once, so early departure forfeits the unearned share.

SAFE / convertible note

Early-stage funding instruments that convert into equity later, at a future valuation.

Pivot

A deliberate, evidence-based change in the product, market, or model — not the same as giving up.

Product-market fit

The point where enough real customers want your product enough that growth stops being a struggle.

TAM / SAM / SOM

Total, Serviceable, and Obtainable market size — three shrinking circles that show how big an opportunity realistically is for you.

Before you go

Questions founders always ask

Do I need a working prototype to enter a competition?

No — most institutional formats fund prototype development after selection. What you need going in is a well-argued proposal: a real problem, a credible solution, and evidence you understand your users.

What if my idea doesn't make it past the institution round?

Take the evaluator feedback literally, fix the weakest-scoring criterion first, and re-enter next cycle. Very few funded ventures win their very first pitch.

Can I use grant money to buy myself a laptop?

Only if the laptop is a genuinely necessary tool for the funded project and that's disclosed and approved in your plan — general personal equipment is routinely treated as a non-permissible expense.

Do I need to register a company before I have a customer?

Usually no. Validate and make your first few sales as an individual or informal team, then formalise the structure once revenue, funding, or risk genuinely require it — see Station 05.

Who actually owns the idea and any resulting company?

This varies by programme — read the fine print on IP ownership before you sign anything, and ask your mentor or institution directly if a call for proposals doesn't spell it out.

I'm not technical. Can I still be a founder?

Yes. Teams that pair a domain-problem expert with a builder consistently out-perform solo technical founders with no user insight. Find your complement early.
